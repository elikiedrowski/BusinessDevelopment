# Strategic Context & Competitive Positioning

## The industry as it stands

The public adjusting industry sits between consumers and insurance carriers. Paymon's view from inside Noble: PAs have **no actual legal power**. They can't compel an insurance company to pay anything — they can only build a paper trail that an attorney could later use in litigation. Most of the "work" desk adjusters do is templated copy-paste correspondence designed to demonstrate contact volume.

Insurance industry tactics (Paymon's framing):

- ~$2T/year in premiums collected; whatever isn't paid out is profit
- Industry strategy historically: **"deny, delay, defend"** (Paymon described this as an insurance-industry motto associated with State Farm in the 80s/90s; verify before using externally)
- Initial settlement offers can be dramatically below what the policy entitles the homeowner to; Paymon used "below 10%" as a broad directional example
- Most homeowners take the first offer because they don't know any better and the negotiation drags for months

## Target market

- Paymon's estimate: ~99% of public-adjusting client-side activity is ordinary homeowners / property owners, often in the ~$200–400k property range
- These homeowners face two structural gaps:
  - **Too small for a PA** — PAs need a claim large enough that 10–20% is worth their effort
  - **Too small for an attorney** — typically need a 6-figure claim before they'll take it on contingency
- Result: a huge population of homeowners with legitimate claims who have **nowhere to turn** and end up just accepting the carrier's lowball offer.

## Competitive landscape — Bo's "Turboax"

Surfaced via Eli's lunch with Valerie after the Paymon/Eli dinner. Bo (Noble's owner) is reportedly close to launching an AI-branded product called "Turboax".

- **What it is:** An extension of public adjusting. Still anchored to the PA contract / fee model. Still likely requires PA certification per state if Noble is representing the consumer. Paymon's read: it's a derivative of ideas he originally pitched Bo, but watered down.
- **What it isn't:** Disruptive. It still depends on becoming a PA contract. The AI is a thin layer over the existing fee model.

## Our positioning

A **disruptive consumer-facing platform** that:

1. Replaces the public adjuster role for the average homeowner
2. Is positioned as a **software tool** (not a representative) — TurboTax model, not CPA model
3. Monetizes B2B (lead gen to attorneys, contractors, services) rather than consumer fees
4. Captures the abandoned middle market that PAs and attorneys both ignore

## Why the software-tool framing matters strategically

Beyond regulatory protection (covered in [04 - Technology Scope.md](04%20-%20Technology%20Scope.md)), the framing is the **competitive moat against Bo**:

- Bo can't follow us into the disruptive model without cannibalizing Noble's PA revenue
- Our economics (B2B lead gen) scale per disaster event; Bo's economics (% of claim) require labor-intensive case management
- We can be national from day one (no per-state PA cert); Bo has to expand state-by-state

## Risk: insurance industry as competitor

Paymon's call-out: the **insurance industry itself** is the most credible threat. They may attempt to acquire and shelf the platform to remove it from the market. This shapes:

- Capitalization strategy (don't take carrier money)
- Moat construction (defensible data, partner relationships)
- Long-term governance
