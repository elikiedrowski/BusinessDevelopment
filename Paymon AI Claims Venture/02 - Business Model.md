# Business Model

> Owner: **Paymon** (industry/marketing) and **Lauren** (operating model). Eli's input is on the technology required to support each revenue stream.

## Revenue model in one line

**Free or near-free for consumers; revenue from B2B lead generation to attorneys, contractors, and services that need high-intent disaster-event leads.**

## Consumer side

- Free, or a low fee (~$200) if a paid tier is needed for unit economics
- Self-service first; human-assisted upsell available
- Optional paid add-ons that may emerge:
  - Matterport scan coordination (~$250 cost basis)
  - Premium document review by a human PA or attorney
  - Concierge claim management for users who want to outsource

## B2B side (the real business)

The platform creates a stream of homeowners with **high-intent, time-sensitive demand** — exactly the audience these businesses pay heavily to reach:

| Buyer | What they want | Why they pay |
|---|---|---|
| **Personal injury / insurance attorneys** | Pre-qualified claim leads | Multi-million-dollar marketing budgets; always starved for clients with documented claims |
| **Roofers** | Storm/hail damage homeowners | Roof replacement is the highest-frequency claim category |
| **HVAC contractors** | Water/smoke damage homes | Rebuild work after losses |
| **Drywall / general contractors** | Interior reconstruction | Same as above |
| **Public adjusters (fallback)** | Cases too complex for self-service | Potential fallback if the user opts out of self-service; regulatory and positioning risk must be evaluated |

## Pricing structure for B2B (TBD)

Two viable models — Phase 2 decision:

1. **Per-lead bidding (Google/Angie's analog)** — partners bid for leads in their geo + service category
2. **Subscription + revenue share** — flat access fee + commission on closed deals

Paymon's existing law firm relationships are the warm channel. He believes they would buy in quickly based on his reputation and the pain of acquiring qualified claim leads. This is a strong hypothesis, but it still needs pre-commit conversations before we treat it as validated.

## Customer acquisition

**Disaster-driven ad campaigns** (Paymon's domain — built Noble's lead gen from scratch):

- Facebook + Google ads, geo-targeted to disaster zones in real time
- Hook: *"Before you sign 10–40% of your claim away, talk to us"*
- Concentrated spend during high-demand events (hurricanes, wildfires, hailstorms)

Counter-positioning vs. PAs in the ad creative is the central marketing wedge.

## Unit economics — open questions

These need to be modeled in Phase 2:

- CAC during peak disaster events vs. quiet periods
- Revenue per acquired user (B2B leads sold per user, blended)
- Conversion rate from "started claim" to "completed claim with B2B referral"
- Take rate the partners will accept
- Volume needed to be sustainable vs. seasonally lumpy

## Critical assumption

This entire model rests on **traffic at the moment of disaster**. The product can be perfect, but if we can't intercept users in the panic window post-event, we don't have a business. Paymon's marketing capability is what makes this real — without that side, we don't build the product.
